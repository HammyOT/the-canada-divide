# Three long-range CSV datasets for interactive charts on Canada’s generational divide

## Executive summary

This report specifies three chart-ready CSV datasets (1990–present where feasible) designed to make **generational divergence** visible in interactive scrollytelling: **housing affordability**, **wages vs prices**, and **education costs & student debt**. Each dataset is built from **primary Canadian sources** (or the closest available official alternative) and comes with (1) a tidy CSV schema suitable for charting libraries, (2) exact source tables/series IDs and citation lines, (3) reproducible transformation steps including **inflation adjustment to 2023 dollars**, (4) a concise data-quality note, (5) recommended chart types + mock captions, and (6) a 10-row CSV preview snippet.

A key methodological choice throughout is to show the generational divide using **“same-age, different-year”** comparisons: e.g., *25–34-year-olds in 1990* vs *25–34-year-olds today* represent very different birth cohorts, which allows you to visualize how the economic “starting line” has shifted. The report includes a standard **cohort mapping** (Gen Z + Millennials vs Gen X + Boomers) you can apply consistently across all three datasets.

These datasets are intentionally aligned to plug into the “chart slots” and claims structure you’re building for your scrollytelling piece (as suggested in your working draft). fileciteturn0file0

## Dataset A housing affordability and price-to-income gap

### Purpose and story use

This dataset supports two complementary housing narratives:

1) **Affordability pressure over time** using the central bank’s **housing affordability index** (share of disposable income required, based on typical mortgage terms). citeturn1view1turn46search0  
2) **Price-to-income gap by age** using an official housing price index (NHPI) and age-specific income series, converted into a single “gap index” that tells you whether housing prices have outpaced incomes for “young adult” vs “mid-career” groups.

NHPI is an official index of contractors’ selling prices for **new** houses (not resale), with national, provincial, and CMA coverage (including **Toronto** and **Vancouver** CMAs). citeturn30search3

### Frequency, coverage, units

- **Frequency:** Quarterly recommended (because the affordability index is quarterly). NHPI is monthly → convert to quarterly average. citeturn1view1turn33view0  
- **Coverage:** 1990Q1–2024Q4 (affordability series extends; some income components may end earlier depending on the selected income table’s latest year). citeturn46search0turn5view1  
- **Geography:** Canada + one CMA (recommended: Toronto CMA; optionally add Vancouver CMA). citeturn30search3turn5view1  
- **Units:** 
  - `boc_hai` = ratio (share of disposable income; e.g., 0.50 = 50%). citeturn1view1turn46search0  
  - `nhpi` = index (official NHPI base period shown in the table, and re-indexed to 1990=100 for comparability). citeturn30search3  
  - `income_nominal_cad` / `income_real_2023_cad` = dollars and constant dollars (CPI-adjusted). citeturn5view1turn33view1  
  - `pti_gap_index_1990_100` = dimensionless index (100 in 1990; >100 means house prices rose faster than incomes).

### Reproducible transformation steps and pseudo-code

Key logic: NHPI is an index, while incomes are in dollars; to produce a clean **price-to-income gap** measure you (a) inflate-adjust income, (b) re-index both series to 1990=100, then (c) compute the ratio of indexes.

```text
INPUTS
- boc_hai_q: series INDINF_AFFORD_Q (quarterly)
- nhpi_m: StatCan 18-10-0205-01 (monthly), select Total (house+land), geos: Canada, Toronto CMA (optional Vancouver CMA)
- income_y: StatCan 11-10-0239-01 (annual), select:
    * sex = both sexes
    * age_groups = 25-34, 35-44, 45-54
    * metric = average income (excluding zeros) [or your chosen consistent income statistic]
    * income_source = wages and salaries (or total income; document your choice)
    * geos = Canada, Toronto CMA (optional Vancouver CMA)
- cpi_y: StatCan 18-10-0005-01 (annual), all-items, Canada

STEP 1: DATE STANDARDIZATION
- Convert nhpi monthly dates to quarter labels; aggregate to quarterly average (mean).
- Convert income annual year to quarters by carrying the annual value across 4 quarters
  (or linear interpolation; set a flag "income_interpolated=true" if you interpolate).

STEP 2: INFLATION ADJUSTMENT (CPI base year 2023)
- For each year y, compute deflator_y = CPI_2023 / CPI_y
- income_real_2023 = income_nominal * deflator_y

STEP 3: RE-INDEX TO 1990=100
- nhpi_index_1990 = nhpi / nhpi_value_at_1990Q1 * 100   (or 1990 annual avg if annual)
- income_index_1990 = income_real_2023 / income_real_2023_at_1990 * 100

STEP 4: PRICE-TO-INCOME GAP INDEX
- pti_gap_index_1990_100 = (nhpi_index_1990 / income_index_1990) * 100

STEP 5: OUTPUT TIDY CSV
- Stack into long form rows with columns:
  (period, geo, age_group, metric, value, unit, source_id, notes)
```

This approach preserves interpretability: a rising `pti_gap_index_1990_100` means prices have outpaced incomes since 1990 for that age group and geography.

### Data-quality note

- NHPI measures **new home** selling prices (contractors), not resale; results can diverge from resale-based measures in some cycles. citeturn33view0turn30search3  
- The affordability index relies on model assumptions (mortgage terms and typical down payment among others), so it is best interpreted as a consistent **benchmark** rather than a household-specific experience. citeturn1view1turn46search0  
- Income by age group for CMAs depends on the underlying income survey series over time (survey/program changes are a known source of comparability issues in long-run income tables). citeturn30search1turn5view1  
- Quarterly “income” values are either carried forward from annual or interpolated; annotate this clearly in the CSV.

## Dataset B real wage growth versus consumer prices by age

### Purpose and story use

The backbone of the wage series here is a long-run **real hourly wage index** by age group published in an official analytical product, with explicit notes about interpolation for some early years and the underlying survey sources. citeturn8view0

### Frequency, coverage, units

### Reproducible transformation steps and pseudo-code

```text
INPUTS
- wage_index_age_sex_y: from the embedded "Data table for chart 2" (men) and "chart 3" (women)
  * select age groups: 25-34, 35-44, 45-54
  * use years 1990..2024
- cpi_y: StatCan 18-10-0005-01 all-items, Canada (annual)

STEP 1: EXTRACT WAGE TABLES
- Parse the HTML page and extract the chart tables into a tidy structure:
  (year, age_group, sex, real_wage_index_1981_100)

STEP 2: RE-INDEX REAL WAGES TO 1990=100
- For each (age_group, sex):
    real_wage_index_1990 = (real_wage_index_1981 / value_in_1990) * 100

STEP 3: CPI SERIES (TWO OUTPUTS)
- CPI_1990_index: cpi_index_1990 = (CPI_y / CPI_1990) * 100
- CPI_2023_deflator: deflator_y = CPI_2023 / CPI_y  (for converting nominal → 2023 dollars elsewhere)

STEP 4: COHORT MAPPING (SAME-AGE CROSS-YEAR)
- birth_year_min = year - age_max
- birth_year_max = year - age_min
- Map birth years to cohort labels:
    Boomers: 1946–1964
    Gen X:   1965–1980
    Millennials: 1981–1996
    Gen Z:   1997–2012
- cohort_bucket:
    younger = Millennials + Gen Z
    older   = Boomers + Gen X
    mixed   = otherwise (range spans both)
```

The wage publication explicitly notes that certain early-year values are interpolations and documents its survey lineage over time, which should be included in your methodology footer if you scrape/republish the index. citeturn8view0

### Data-quality note

- The wage series is a **real wage index** (already inflation-adjusted) and includes explicitly stated interpolations for some early years; it is best treated as a consistent long-run indicator rather than a direct replacement for microdata-based median wage series. citeturn8view0  
- When charting “real wage index” beside “CPI index,” clarify that CPI is presented as a **price-level index**, while the wage index reflects **purchasing power** (already deflated); the point of the combined view is narrative contrast, not arithmetic equivalence.

## Dataset C education costs and student debt

### Purpose and story use

This dataset links two dynamics that are central to generational inequality narratives:

- **Education sticker price** (tuition fees) rising over decades, inflation-adjusted to show real growth.  
- **Debt burden at graduation**, available in official tables at **five-year intervals**, suitable for a scrollytelling dot/step overlay.

A crucial technical detail: tuition series requires **stitching** an archived historical table (through 2006/2007) with the current table (from 2006/2007 onward). citeturn22view1turn23view0

### Frequency, coverage, units

- **Tuition:** Annual by academic year; recommended coverage is **1990/1991 through 2024/2025** by stitching the two official tables. citeturn23view0turn22view1

### Reproducible transformation steps and pseudo-code

```text
INPUTS
- tuition_hist: 37-10-0160-01 (1972/73..2006/07)
- tuition_curr: 37-10-0045-01 (2006/07..latest)
- debt_5yr: 37-10-0036-01 (2000..2020 every 5 years)
- cpi_y: 18-10-0005-01 (annual CPI, all-items, Canada)

STEP 1: FILTER TO YOUR CORE SERIES
- tuition fields:
    geo = Canada
    level = undergraduate (Canadian students, full-time) [document exact table filter]
- debt fields:
    geo/province = Canada (or keep province dimension for drilldown)
    debt_source = all sources
    level_of_study = bachelor/undergraduate (or your chosen consistent level)

STEP 2: STANDARDIZE ACADEMIC YEAR
- Parse academic year strings "1990 / 1991" → academic_year_start=1990
- Keep academic_year_label for display

STEP 3: STITCH TUITION SERIES
- Combine tuition_hist and tuition_curr
- For overlap year 2006/2007:
    choose tuition_curr as authoritative (recommended because it’s the active program)
    OR choose tuition_hist if you want historical continuity
- Add column: tuition_stitch_source = "hist" or "curr"

STEP 4: INFLATION ADJUSTMENT TO 2023 CAD
- Use CPI_2023 as base.
- For academic_year_start=y:
    tuition_real_2023 = tuition_nominal * (CPI_2023 / CPI_y)

STEP 5: OPTIONAL INDEXING FOR VISUALS

- debt_index_2000_100 = debt_real_2023 / debt_real_2023_at_2000 * 100

STEP 6: OUTPUT LONG/TIDY CSV
- One row per (academic_year_start, geo, metric)
- Keep missing years for debt as blank (best) rather than interpolating
```

### Data-quality note

- Tuition series includes documented corrections in some academic years; if you reproduce or publish a stitched series, include a short “data revisions” note in your methodology panel. citeturn22view1

- Academic-year tuition deflation uses annual CPI as an approximation; if you want higher fidelity, deflate using the average CPI across the two calendar years spanned by the academic year (e.g., mean of CPI_1990 and CPI_1991 for 1990/1991).

## Shared reproducibility recipes

### Inflation adjustment to 2023 dollars

Use CPI annual average (all-items) so every money series in your project uses the same base-year conversion. citeturn33view1turn16search0

```text
Given:
- CPI_y: CPI annual average for year y
- CPI_2023: CPI annual average for 2023

Deflator_y = CPI_2023 / CPI_y
Real_2023 = Nominal_y * Deflator_y
```

If your denominator/numerator is academic-year (e.g., tuition 1990/1991), choose the academic-year start year’s CPI or the mean of the two years, and document the rule.

### Cohort mapping and cohort buckets

To turn age groups into generational buckets in a **same-age cross-year** chart:

```text
birth_year_min = year - age_max
birth_year_max = year - age_min

Cohorts (standard, document once in methodology):
- Boomers: 1946–1964
- Gen X: 1965–1980
- Millennials: 1981–1996
- Gen Z: 1997–2012

Bucket:
- younger = Millennials + Gen Z
- older   = Boomers + Gen X
- mixed   = overlap range crosses buckets
```

Use `mixed` sparingly (e.g., grey band), or split the row into fractional cohort shares if you want maximum precision.

### Interpolation and aggregation rules

- **Monthly → quarterly:** average (mean) is usually best for indices. citeturn33view0  
- **Annual → quarterly:** carry-forward is preferable to interpolation unless you clearly label “interpolated.”  
- **Five-year student-debt series:** avoid interpolation in the published chart; show points and annotate “five-year intervals.” citeturn22view0  

## Gaps and limitations

- **No single perfect “house price to household income” metro series** exists in official tables back to 1990 with quarterly frequency; the recommended workaround is (a) official affordability index for the national benchmark, plus (b) NHPI (metro) combined with a consistent income series and re-indexed gap measures. citeturn1view1turn33view0turn5view1  
- **Student debt** is not annual in the main official series; treat it as structural “milestone” data points rather than a smooth trend line. citeturn22view0  
- **Tuition** requires stitching archived and current tables; always note the stitch year and correction history in your methodology panel. citeturn22view1turn23view0  

## Implementation checklist for interactive charts

- Put the three output CSVs in a `data/derived/` folder and keep a `data/sources/README.md` that lists the exact table/series IDs, download links, and your transformation rules (inflation base year, stitch year, interpolation policy). citeturn14view1turn38search5  
- For housing and wages, default the UI to **1990=100** indexing with a toggle for “show in original units” where appropriate.
