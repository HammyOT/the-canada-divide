# Born Too Late: Canada's Generational Divide

**Investigative Series · March 2026**
**By The Research Desk · Updated March 2026**

*Housing, wages, and wealth: how Canada's economic promise became conditional on when you were born — and what the data actually shows.*

---

## A Different Canada, By Age

**STAT: Homeownership at ages 25–29 → 36.5%**
*Down from 44.1% in 2011. Source: Statistics Canada (NHS/Census 2011–2021)*

For much of the twentieth century, Canada promised each generation that hard work would produce a better life than the one before. Stable employment, accessible housing, and affordable education formed the foundation of what many Canadians saw as a reliable social contract.

For many young Canadians today, that contract appears increasingly fragile. Across the country, younger generations are entering adulthood facing housing prices far out of reach of their incomes, rising living costs, and higher levels of student debt than previous cohorts. While older generations often built wealth through homeownership during decades of rising asset prices, younger Canadians must now navigate an economic environment where the entry barriers to wealth accumulation are dramatically higher.

This generational divide is not simply the result of bad luck or individual choices. It is deeply connected to political decisions about housing policy, taxation, labour markets, and social supports — and it is most clearly visible when you compare the same age groups across different points in time.

The most rigorous way to measure this gap is to compare cohorts at the same life stage. Homeownership among 30–34-year-olds in 2011 versus 2021, for instance. When you do, the picture is stark. And it extends well beyond housing.

## Housing Split the Generations

**STAT: Homeownership at ages 30–34 → 52.3%**
*Down from 59.2% in 2011. Source: Statistics Canada (NHS/Census 2011–2021)*

Statistics Canada's comparison of homeownership across three consecutive Censuses offers one of the cleanest generational comparisons available — and one of the most damning. Among Canadians aged 25–29, the ownership rate fell from 44.1% in 2011 to just 36.5% in 2021. The 30–34 group dropped from 59.2% to 52.3% over the same decade.

By contrast, the homeownership rate for Canadians aged 70–74 held nearly constant: 75.5%, 75.8%, and 74.8% across those same three Census years. This is not a lifecycle story. The oldest cohorts stayed put. The youngest fell behind — at the exact ages where previous generations had already formed households, purchased homes, and begun accumulating equity.

Several forces converged to produce this outcome. Housing supply in growing urban regions failed to keep pace with demand. Zoning regulations restricted higher-density development. Population growth and immigration increased demand. Low interest rates through much of the 2010s expanded borrowing capacity — but also inflated prices. And housing became increasingly treated as an investment asset rather than a place to live.

In cities such as Toronto and Vancouver, housing prices grew several times faster than wages over the past two decades. Canada's overall homeownership rate was 66.5% in 2021 — ranking 23rd among OECD countries, well below the OECD average of 71.5%.

> **PULL QUOTE:** "The divide is not only young versus old — it is young with family assets versus young without."

A particularly striking finding from Statistics Canada underscores the compounding nature of this gap: people born in the 1990s whose parents were homeowners were about twice as likely to own in 2021 as those whose parents were not homeowners. Parental multiple-property ownership is associated with even higher relative likelihoods of the child owning a home. The housing gap is becoming self-replicating across generations.

CMHC has repeatedly argued that restoring affordability requires a major increase in housing supply. Its 2022 work projected an additional 3.5 million units needed by 2030 to restore affordability. A later estimate from the Parliamentary Budget Officer offered a lower figure — roughly 1.3 million units — attributing the discrepancy to different definitions of what "affordable" means. The estimates diverge, but the direction is the same: Canada is not building nearly enough.

## Rent Took the Slack

**STAT: Renters spending 30%+ of income on shelter → 33.0%**
*vs. 16.1% for owners. Source: Statistics Canada, Canadian Housing Survey 2022*

Statistics Canada defines "unaffordable housing" as spending 30% or more of household income on shelter costs. In 2022, 22.0% of all Canadian households met that definition. But the burden falls unevenly: renters (33.0%) are more than twice as likely as owners (16.1%) to face unaffordable shelter costs — a gap Statistics Canada describes as persistent over time.

The situation is compounded for those entering the rental market now. The Canadian Housing Survey reported that "new renters" — those who recently moved — pay substantially higher rents than longer-tenure renters, reflecting the gap between rents locked in under existing leases and rents on newly available units. In tightly constrained markets, the cost of moving is, effectively, the cost of paying market price.

Higher rent leaves less room for saving, for schooling, and for starting a family. For many young Canadians, the question is not whether to rent or own — it is how long to rent before any path to ownership becomes conceivable. That horizon keeps moving further away.

First-time homebuyers' median outstanding mortgage rose from $249,350 in 2018 to $280,000 in 2022 — a 12% jump in just four years. Incomes did not rise nearly as fast. The financial threshold to enter ownership keeps rising, making each year of renting simultaneously more expensive and less of a savings opportunity.

## Pay Did Not Keep Up

**STAT: Real median hourly wage growth, 1981–2024 → +20%**
*Full-time jobs: +24%. Part-time jobs: +6%. Source: Statistics Canada Labour Force Survey*

Statistics Canada reports that from 1981 to 2024, median real hourly wages grew 20% overall — roughly half a percentage point per year in inflation-adjusted terms. That is modest, but it exists. The more important story is in the distribution, both across time and across age groups.

From 1981 to 1998, wage growth for 25–34-year-olds lagged 45–54-year-olds by roughly 20 percentage points. From 1998 to 2024, the younger group partially recovered — about 5 percentage points faster growth than the older cohort — but that recovery barely offsets the earlier divergence, and it coincided with years of rapid housing price appreciation.

Youth unemployment tells another part of the story. Recent Labour Force Survey releases show youth unemployment spiking in 2025 and remaining elevated into early 2026 relative to other age groups. Job quality matters too: Statistics Canada's "Quality of Employment" reporting documents long-run trends in involuntary part-time work from 1997 to 2022, showing that the problem is not only job quantity but job stability and earnings reliability.

Even small wage gaps compound over time. Essentials rise; paychecks lag; the savings required to cross the threshold into homeownership keep receding. For workers who entered the labour market after 2000 in major urban centres — precisely the cohort that faced housing price acceleration — the timing has been doubly difficult.

## The Starting Line Moved

**STAT: Average undergrad tuition (2022/23) → $6,834**
*Projected $7,734 for 2025/26. Source: Statistics Canada*

Statistics Canada reports average tuition for full-time Canadian undergraduates at $6,834 in 2022/2023, with projections reaching $7,734 for 2025/2026. In real (inflation-adjusted) terms, tuition has climbed faster than consumer prices across most of the past three decades — a structural shift in the cost of entry to credentialed adulthood.

For student debt, official data offers two complementary windows: debt at graduation (tracked roughly every five years through the National Graduates Survey) and the stock of outstanding student loans within household balance sheets (tracked through the Survey of Financial Security). Together they paint a picture of young adults entering the housing market and family formation years already carrying significant financial obligations.

One notable policy shift: the federal government permanently eliminated interest on Canada Student Loans effective April 1, 2023. This reduces the ongoing repayment burden for federally-held debt — but does not change principal balances, does not address provincial loan portions or private borrowing, and does not reduce the broader affordability constraints facing young adults.

**INFOBOX: Intergenerational Mobility — Context**

Statistics Canada reports that for adult children born in 1985, approximately 57% had higher family income than their parents at the same age — Canada's rate of absolute intergenerational income mobility. By comparison: the U.S. sits at 50%, the U.K. at 65%, Norway at 74%. Canada performs moderately, but earlier research suggests relative mobility (rank persistence across generations) may be declining — a warning sign.

---

For many, adulthood begins with five figures of debt. Delayed milestones — moving out, family formation, regional mobility — follow naturally. The connection to the housing story is direct: student debt reduces the savings capacity needed for down payments, and the years spent repaying debt are years not spent accumulating equity. These channels interact and reinforce each other across the decade of a young adult's life.

## The Wealth Gap Runs Deep

**STAT: Median net worth, under-35 households (2023) → $159,100**
*55–64 cohort: $873,400. Source: Statistics Canada Survey of Financial Security, constant 2023 dollars*

The Survey of Financial Security provides an unusually strong long-run view of median net worth by age, reported in constant 2023 dollars. In 1999, the median net worth of a household with a major income recipient under 35 was $30,000. By 2023, that had risen to $159,100 — a dramatic percentage gain, driven largely by asset price movements including housing.

But the absolute comparison tells a different story. The 55–64 age group held median net worth of $873,400 in 2023, up from $457,300 in 1999. The 35–44 group went from $161,200 to $409,300. The gap between age groups did not close; in many respects it widened. And these are averages across each cohort — within-generation inequality, particularly between those with and without family property wealth, is significant.

Interpretation requires care. Net worth is strongly lifecycle-driven — older households typically have higher wealth because they have had more years to accumulate it. The most rigorous generational claims compare cohorts at the same age across different calendar periods. When Statistics Canada researchers do that for Canadian data, the evidence consistently shows that younger cohorts have faced steeper entry barriers to wealth accumulation than older cohorts did at the same age.

> **PULL QUOTE:** "In 1999, the median under-35 household had $30,000 in net worth. By 2023, it was $159,100 — a gain driven almost entirely by housing assets, which most young renters don't have."

## What Policy Can Do

No single lever closes the generational gap. Here are the main options being debated, with what the evidence says about each — and where the trade-offs lie.

**1. Accelerate Housing Supply**
Zoning reform, faster approvals, and infrastructure financing to enable significantly more construction. CMHC estimates Canada needs annual housing starts approaching 430,000–480,000 units over the next decade — nearly double the current pace — to return to 2019 affordability levels.
*Trade-off: Long lags between policy change and new supply; construction capacity constraints; local political resistance; geographic mismatch if supply occurs where demand is weakest.*

**2. Scale Deeply Affordable Housing**
Coordinated multi-level government investment in non-market and deeply affordable housing, targeting the households most rent-burdened. The IRPP argues this segment is systematically under-targeted by market-rate supply increases.
*Trade-off: Requires sustained public funding and delivery capacity; can be slow without ready land and building pipelines.*

**3. Address Investor Demand**
Data collection, taxes, and rules targeting investor-owned residential properties. Statistics Canada reports that in dense condo tracts, roughly half of condominiums were rented by investors in 2020–2021, raising questions about how financialization of housing affects availability and prices.
*Trade-off: Design complexity; may reduce rental supply if bluntly applied; enforcement and data challenges.*

**4. Caution on Demand-Side Purchase Subsidies**
The Bank of Canada has repeatedly warned that restoring affordability requires supply-demand balance, and that purchase subsidies risk inflating prices in supply-constrained markets. The First Home Savings Account (FHSA) supports first-time buyers' savings, but broad buyer subsidies without matching supply risk benefiting sellers more than buyers.
*Trade-off: Politically difficult; doesn't directly help liquidity-constrained buyers; may add to price pressure in hot markets.*

**5. Income and Job Quality Supports**
Wage policy, job quality initiatives, and supports targeting involuntary part-time work and youth underemployment. Addresses the affordability gap from the income side, and helps both renters and aspiring owners.
*Trade-off: Wage growth alone is unlikely to catch up to asset price growth already accumulated; inflation interaction; sectoral and regional differences.*

**6. Reduce Education Cost Growth and Debt Burden**
Building on the April 2023 federal interest elimination on Canada Student Loans, further reforms could reduce principal burdens or expand grants. Lower debt loads entering adulthood improve household balance sheets and early savings capacity.
*Trade-off: Does not solve the housing supply gap; tuition is only one part of total student cost — living costs are rising too.*
