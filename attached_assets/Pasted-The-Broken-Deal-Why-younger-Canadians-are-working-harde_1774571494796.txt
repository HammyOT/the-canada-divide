The Broken Deal
Why younger Canadians are working harder, waiting longer, and owning less
For decades, Canada sold a familiar promise.
Study hard. Find work. Rent your first place. Save up. Maybe buy a home. Build a stable life that is a little more secure than the one before it.
That promise was never equally available to everyone. It was shaped by class, race, immigration status, region, and luck. But for many Canadians, it still felt possible.
For a growing share of younger adults, it no longer does.
Across housing, wages, and education costs, the numbers point in the same direction: younger Canadians are entering adulthood in a more expensive and more unequal economy. The distance between effort and stability has widened. Milestones that once defined middle-class security now arrive later, cost more, or remain out of reach altogether.
The pressure shows up most clearly in housing.
Statistics Canada comparisons of Canadians at the same age over time show that homeownership has fallen sharply for younger adults. Among people aged 25 to 29, the homeownership rate dropped from 44.1 percent in 2011 to 36.5 percent in 2021. Among those aged 30 to 34, it fell from 59.2 percent to 52.3 percent. For older Canadians, ownership rates remained much higher and far more stable.
That matters because in Canada, housing is not just shelter. It is one of the country’s most powerful wealth-building tools.
When younger adults are priced out of ownership, they are not simply delayed in buying a home. They are often shut out of the asset that has helped many older households build security, absorb shocks, and pass advantages on to their children. In practice, housing has become the main engine turning price increases into long-term generational inequality.
For renters, the pressure is even more immediate. Statistics Canada defines unaffordable housing as spending 30 percent or more of household income on shelter. In 2022, 22.0 percent of households in Canada met that threshold. But the burden was much heavier for renters: 33.0 percent spent at least 30 percent of income on shelter, compared with 16.1 percent of owners.
That gap helps explain why so many younger Canadians feel stuck in place. High rents make it harder to save. Smaller savings make down payments harder to reach. Delayed homeownership means missing years of equity growth. Over time, the disadvantage compounds.
The result is not just an affordability problem. It is a wealth problem too.
Statistics Canada data shows that median net worth remains far higher for older households than for younger ones. In constant 2023 dollars, households with a major income earner under 35 had a median net worth of $30,000 in 1999 and $159,100 in 2023. For those aged 55 to 64, the figure was $457,300 in 1999 and $873,400 in 2023.
Some of that is expected. Wealth usually rises with age. But the scale of the gap still matters, especially in an economy where housing ownership has become such a strong divider.
The divide is no longer just about growing older and accumulating savings over time. It is increasingly about who managed to enter the asset economy early enough to benefit from it.
That is also why the generational story can be misleading if told too simply. The real divide is not every older person against every younger one. It is also between younger Canadians whose families already have assets and those whose families do not. Framing this only as Millennials versus Boomers misses a deeper reality: inequality also runs within generations, especially between those with access to family wealth and those without.
Work, meanwhile, tells a more complicated story than social media slogans suggest.
Statistics Canada reports that from 1981 to 2024, median real hourly wages grew 20 percent overall, with stronger gains in full-time jobs than in part-time work. Wages rose, but that does not mean younger workers are imagining the squeeze.
A person can earn more in inflation-adjusted terms than someone decades earlier and still be worse off in the places that matter most. If rent absorbs more income, if buying a home takes far longer, and if adult life begins under heavier education costs, modest wage gains do not restore the old bargain. They only make a harsher economy slightly more survivable.
Education shows that clearly.
Statistics Canada reported that average tuition for Canadian undergraduate students is expected to reach $7,734 in the 2025 to 2026 academic year, in June 2025 constant dollars. That is far above the roughly $2,340 inflation-adjusted benchmark often used for 1981 in long-run comparisons.
Put that beside wages at the floor of the labour market. The federal minimum wage is scheduled to rise to $18.15 an hour on April 1, 2026. At that rate, average annual undergraduate tuition alone would require about 426 hours of work, before rent, groceries, books, or transit are even counted.
That is one of the clearest ways to show the shift. The problem is not only that tuition is high. It is that the amount of time needed to pay for it has grown while other essentials have become harder to afford at the same time.
It is tempting to look for one obvious cause or one group to blame. But problems this large are rarely that simple. Canada’s generational squeeze did not emerge from a single decision, a single law, or a single demographic change. It built up over time as housing supply lagged, homes became investment assets, wages struggled to keep pace with key costs, and public policy often protected existing asset holders more than new entrants.
Part of the answer is supply. Canada did not build enough housing in the places where demand grew fastest. Zoning limits, approval delays, infrastructure bottlenecks, and weak construction capacity all helped leave too few homes chasing too many buyers and renters.
Population growth can increase pressure in already tight housing markets, but it does not by itself explain why governments allowed shortages, weak rental protections, and speculative housing dynamics to build for so long. Blaming immigrants alone for a decades-long affordability problem confuses an added source of demand with the deeper policy and market failures that made the system so brittle in the first place.
But supply is only part of the story. Housing also became a powerful investment asset. Cheap credit, investor demand, and the treatment of homes as appreciating financial vehicles helped push prices beyond what wages alone could support.
The squeeze also reflects the weakening of supports that once made early adult life easier to navigate. More affordable education, stronger public supports, and more stable entry-level work once made it easier for younger adults to establish themselves without relying as heavily on family wealth or asset appreciation. As those cushions weakened, the risks of adulthood became more individualized.
These forces differ, but they point to the same conclusion: the current divide is not a natural law. It is the product of political and economic choices.
That is why the frustration surrounding generational inequality is not just cultural bitterness or online complaining. It reflects a deeper fear that the rules of economic life have changed. Work still matters, but ownership matters more. Credentials still matter, but inheritance matters too. Adulthood is increasingly shaped not just by what you earn, but by when you enter the market, what your family can help with, and whether you can hold on long enough to catch up.
The danger is not only economic. It is political.
When a society cannot explain its own economic pressures clearly, it becomes easier to blame the wrong people. Immigrants, environmental rules, younger people’s spending habits, or older generations as a whole can all become convenient targets. But scapegoats do not explain why homes were allowed to function as wealth machines, why supply remained constrained, why rents rose so sharply, or why the first rungs of adult life became so expensive. Simpler stories may feel satisfying. They are also less true.
That also means the solutions will not be simple. But the broad directions are clear.
On housing, Canada can expand supply, invest in affordable and non-market housing, and limit speculative investment. Those reforms matter, but they take time and face real political and design challenges.
On income, governments can strengthen wage and income supports to improve affordability for renters and younger adults. Those measures can ease the pressure, even if they cannot fully offset rising asset prices on their own.
On education, governments can reduce tuition growth and student debt burdens to make the transition into adulthood more affordable. That would help, but it still would not solve broader housing pressures by itself.
Still, one thing is already clear. If younger Canadians are working, studying, and saving while falling further behind on the most basic measures of security, then this is not just a story about personal budgeting or unrealistic expectations. It is a story about a country where the old promise has weakened.
And when that weakening is not explained clearly, it becomes easier to blame the wrong people instead of confronting the deeper structure of the problem.
For many young adults, the result is simple and harsh: the milestones that once defined a stable adult life now take longer, cost more, and increasingly depend on help not everyone has.
