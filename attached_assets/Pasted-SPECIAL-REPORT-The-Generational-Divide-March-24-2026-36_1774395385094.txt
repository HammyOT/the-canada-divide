SPECIAL REPORT
The Generational Divide
March 24, 2026

36.5%
Homeownership at ages 25–29 in 2021, down from 44.1% in 2011.

For decades, the unspoken Canadian promise was simple: get a good education, work hard, save a portion of your paycheck, and you will eventually achieve financial security. But for younger Canadians today, doing everything "right" no longer guarantees that outcome. The path into adulthood hasn't just hit a speedbump; it has fundamentally changed.

If the country is richer, why does it feel harder?

Define the Question
When we talk about a "generational divide," the conversation often gets derailed by misunderstandings. It is easy to look at a 60-year-old and a 30-year-old today and say, "Of course the older person has more wealth—they've been working longer." But that is the wrong way to measure fairness.

The most accurate test of generational equality is not comparing young versus old in a single snapshot of time. It is comparing people at the exact same stage of life across different eras. We have to ask: what did life look like for a 30-year-old in 1995, compared to a 30-year-old today?

When we look through this "same age, different era" lens, the data reveals a stark reality. Younger Canadians are not just delaying adulthood; the traditional milestones of economic security have been moved significantly further out of reach. At the center of this shift is the very foundation of middle-class wealth: housing.

What changed first: the housing ladder, or the rules around it?

Housing Split the Generations
52.3%
Homeownership at ages 30–34 in 2021, down from 59.2% in 2011.

At the exact same ages when earlier cohorts were buying their first homes, younger Canadians are increasingly locked out. The numbers show a clear and steady decline in young homeownership over the last decade. But crucially, this drop is not happening across the board.

While homeownership rates for older demographics—like those aged 70–74—have remained broadly stable and comfortably high, younger cohorts are losing ground. This proves that the decline isn't just a natural part of growing up, nor is it the result of younger people choosing different lifestyles.

The housing ladder itself has fundamentally transformed. The bottom rungs have been removed, creating a deep economic fault line. Today, housing heavily favors those who were lucky enough to be born early enough to buy in before prices detached from local incomes.

Ages 25–29: 44.1% (2011) → 36.5% (2021)

Ages 30–34: 59.2% (2011) → 52.3% (2021)

Ages 70–74: 75.5% (2011) → 74.8% (2021)

Did timing become destiny?

Rent Took the Slack
33.0%
Share of renters spending 30%+ of income on shelter, versus 16.1% of owners.

A common argument is that if young people cannot buy, they can simply rent and save the difference. However, the data shows that renting is no longer a safe financial fallback. Younger adults are being pushed into a hyper-competitive rental market that consumes a massive, often unsustainable, share of their monthly income.

When you spend more than 30% of your income on shelter, it forces difficult compromises on everyday essentials. More importantly, it cripples your ability to save. Renting today delays wealth-building twice over: it drains your current cash flow, and it prevents you from building the down payment required to escape the rental cycle.

With first-time buyers now facing a median outstanding mortgage of $280,000, trying to save for a home while paying modern rental prices is like trying to fill a bathtub with the drain wide open. For many, major life milestones—like getting married, moving out, or having children—are put on indefinite hold.

What does adult life cost now?

Pay Did Not Keep Up
+20%
Real median hourly wage growth, 1981–2024.

When younger generations voice their frustration, they are often met with a simple piece of advice: "Just earn more." But this ignores the mathematical reality of the modern Canadian economy.

It is true that wages have risen over the last four decades. Full-time jobs have seen a 24% increase in real wages, and part-time jobs are up 6%. Paychecks are technically bigger than they were in the 1980s. However, this modest wage growth has been entirely overshadowed by the exploding costs of the things that actually matter for building a life.

The problem isn't that young people aren't making money; it's that the purchasing power of their paychecks regarding housing and basic living costs has collapsed. They are working just as hard—if not harder—in a more educated, more productive economy, yet their pay simply hasn't kept pace with the towering financial barriers in front of them.

Is this a temporary spike, or the new baseline?

The Starting Line Moved
$6,834
Average tuition for full-time Canadian undergraduates in 2022/2023 (Projected $7,734 in 2025/2026).

Post-secondary education has long been sold as the great equalizer and the most reliable ticket to a good career. While a degree still matters deeply in the modern job market, it increasingly comes with an upfront cost that squeezes young adults before their wealth-building years even begin.

Previous generations could often pay for a year of university with savings from a summer job. Today, that mathematical equation is broken. Tuition has climbed significantly faster than inflation across decades. While the permanent elimination of federal student loan interest in 2023 was a positive step that helped ease monthly cash flows, it didn't solve the root issue.

Young workers are entering the economy saddled with massive principal balances while simultaneously navigating historic living-cost pressures. Adult life simply begins with a heavier financial anchor than it ever has before.

What happens when the first step costs more?

The Wealth Gap Runs Deep
$159,100
Median net worth for under-35 households in 2023.

The compounding result of expensive housing, high rent, and debt is a drastically widening wealth gap. Wealth naturally rises with age as people pay down mortgages and invest, but the chasm between today's generations is unprecedented.

Consider this: the median net worth for households aged 55–64 surged from $457,300 in 1999 to a staggering $873,400 in 2023. Over that same period, the under-35 demographic has seen only a fraction of that growth, largely remaining stuck.

The issue is not simply that older people are older. It is that older cohorts benefited from a massive inflation of their housing assets, while younger cohorts are operating in an economy where those exact same assets act as a barricade. Younger Canadians face steeper barriers to building wealth at the exact same life stage as their predecessors.

Is the wealth ladder being pulled up?

Policy Created Feedback Loops

3.5 Million Units
CMHC housing supply gap estimate by 2030 (The PBO estimates 1.3 million).

How did we get here? No single villain or isolated cause explains this generational divide. It is the result of decades of overlapping policy choices, fragmented government decisions, and systemic changes in how we treat real estate.

Supply is a massive piece of the puzzle. We simply have not built enough homes for our growing population, with estimates suggesting we need up to 480,000 new housing starts annually over the next decade. But building alone won't fix everything. Interest rates, mortgage rules, and the financialization of housing all play a role.

When roughly half of the condos in highly-dense downtown areas are scooped up by investors to be rented out for profit, it complicates the market for first-time buyers. Shared jurisdiction between federal, provincial, and municipal governments has created a system where accountability is easily passed around, while the crisis compounds.

Can a policy ever truly be reversed?

The Promise of Mobility
57%
Absolute intergenerational income mobility for Canadians born in 1985.

This data forces us to ask a difficult, foundational question: does Canada still deliver upward mobility? For decades, the promise was that your origins did not dictate your outcomes. But that promise is fracturing.

Today, roughly 4 in 10 Canadians born in 1985 earned less at age 30 than their parents did at the exact same age. Furthermore, family wealth increasingly dictates who gets ahead. Young adults born in the 1990s whose parents were homeowners were about twice as likely to own homes themselves in 2021.

We are shifting toward an economy where access to the "Bank of Mom and Dad" is the primary determinant of whether a young person can achieve economic security. When hard work is outpaced by inheritance and family timing, the basic social contract is broken.

Is Canada still a country where origins do not determine outcomes?

Policy Options
430,000–480,000
Annual housing starts CMHC says are needed over the next decade.

Fixing a divide produced by multiple intersecting systems requires more than one silver bullet. Every policy lever comes with costs, political hurdles, and unintended consequences. A serious approach means looking at the overlapping solutions required to restore balance.

Accelerate Housing Supply: Targets the raw physical shortage of units through zoning reform and construction incentives.

Scale Deeply Affordable Housing: Targets those completely priced out of the private market by building non-market and cooperative housing.

Anti-Financialization Measures: Targets investor demand and speculation to ensure homes are treated as shelter first, not just stock portfolios.

Income and Wage Supports: Targets the gap between earning power and living costs, ensuring work pays enough to live on.

Reduce Education Cost Burden: Targets the debt load carried into early adulthood, freeing up capital for young workers to invest in their futures.

What kind of Canada comes next?

The Canada Divide Is a Political Choice

Generational inequality is not a natural disaster. It is not random, nor is it inevitable. It is the direct reflection of deliberate choices we have made over decades regarding housing, work, education, and what kinds of wealth our society chooses to protect.

Over the last ten years, housing became the definitive hinge issue of Canadian life. The rental market failed to provide a safe harbor, while stagnant purchasing power and rising education costs made entering adulthood demonstrably harder than it was a generation ago. Today, economic security increasingly depends on exactly when you were born and what assets your family holds, rather than just your own labor.

But because this divide was created by policy, it can be corrected by policy. It will require treating the struggles of younger Canadians not as personal failings, but as the structural economic crisis the data proves them to be.

A fair society does not make opportunity depend on whether you arrived before the ladder was pulled up.
